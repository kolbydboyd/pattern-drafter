app layout
Refactor the app layout from a two-column single-page design into a multi-step wizard flow. Create src/ui/wizard.js that manages four steps: 1) CHOOSE — full-screen grid of garment categories (Pants, Shorts, Tops, Skirts, Dresses, Outerwear), clicking a category reveals garments within it, clicking a garment advances to step 2. 2) MEASURE — full-screen measurement form showing only the fields this garment needs, with saved profile dropdown at top, large clear inputs, measurement hints visible, a Next button at the bottom. 3) CUSTOMIZE — full-screen options panel for this garment (fit, pockets, fly, waistband, etc) grouped logically, with a Generate button. 4) PATTERN — full-screen output with a horizontal tab bar at the top: Pieces (SVG renders), Materials (fabric/notions/stitch guide), Instructions (construction steps), Print (paper size selector + print button). Add a stepper/breadcrumb bar fixed at the top showing all 4 steps with the current step highlighted, clickable to navigate back. Each step takes the full viewport width and height. Transition between steps with a simple slide or fade. Move all the current input-building and output-rendering logic from app.js into this wizard flow. Update styles.css — remove the two-column layout, make each step a full-screen view. Keep dark mode toggle in the top-right corner. Make it work well on both desktop and mobile — on mobile the steps stack naturally since each is already full-width.

Referral program. “Give a friend a free pattern, get a free pattern.” Sewing is social — people sew with friends, teach their kids, share in groups. A referral system turns every customer into marketing.
Gift cards. Someone’s mom sews, they don’t know what pattern to get her. A $25 Pattern Drafter gift card is a perfect gift. Easy to implement with Stripe.
Seasonal collections. Every quarter, release a themed batch of patterns — “Summer Linen Collection” with easy pants, camp shirt, A-line skirt. Gives you content to post, email to send, reason for people to come back.
Fit feedback loop. After someone sews a pattern, prompt them: “How did it fit?” with simple options — too tight at chest, too long in torso, crotch too low, etc. Use that data to refine the math over time. This is your unfair advantage — Simplicity can’t do this because they sell fixed sizes. You can literally improve every pattern based on real user feedback because every pattern is generated from math you control.
